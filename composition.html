2. Rule of Thirds

The Rule of Thirds divides your canvas into nine equal parts using two horizontal and two vertical lines. Placing key elements along these lines or at their intersections makes the composition more dynamic.

  • Place the main subject slightly off-center for balance.
  • Use intersections for focal points like faces, trees, or the sun.
  • Avoid putting the subject exactly in the center ‚Äî it can feel flat.

4. Leading Lines

Leading lines guide the viewer‚Äôs eyes through the painting toward the focal point. They can be roads, rivers, branches, or even implied lines between objects.

  • Use lines to direct attention naturally.
  • Diagonal lines create movement and depth.
  • Curved lines give a gentle, flowing effect.

5. Focal Point

The focal point is the area that grabs attention first. Every composition should have a clear point of interest, like a face, bright color, or highlighted object.

  • Use contrast, brightness, or detail to emphasize your focal point.
  • Avoid multiple competing areas of interest.
  • Guide the eye using supporting elements and lines.

6. Depth and Perspective

Creating depth makes a 2D painting feel three-dimensional. You can achieve this using size, color contrast, and overlapping objects.

  • Objects closer to the viewer should be larger and more detailed.
  • Use cooler, lighter colors for background elements.
  • Overlapping creates a sense of space and distance.

7. Contrast and Harmony

Contrast adds energy; harmony adds unity. Combining both makes the composition engaging and cohesive.

  • Contrast: Use light vs dark, rough vs smooth, warm vs cool.
  • Harmony: Repeat colors, shapes, or lines to create consistency.
